Algebra Logic Master Guide

I. Sign Ownership: Who "Owns" the Sign?

In algebra, signs are not just "plus" or "minus" operations; they are part of the number's identity.

The Rules of Sign Ownership:
  1. The "Right-Hand" Rule: A sign always belongs to the number or variable immediately to its right.
  2. The Invisible Positive: If a number is at the start of an expression with no sign, it is secretly Positive (+).
  3. The Bracket Shield: A sign outside a bracket belongs to the entire result of the bracket.
Correct Identification:

\[a - b + c\]

  • The \(a\) is Positive
  • The \(b\) is Negative
  • The \(c\) is Positive
The Trap:

\[10 - 2 + 5\]

Wrong Thinking: Doing \(2 + 5 = 7\) then \(10 - 7 = 3\).

Right Thinking: The minus belongs to the 2. It is \(10 - 2 = 8\), then \(8 + 5 = 13\).

II. How Signs Work (Addition vs. Multiplication)

1. Multiplication & Division (The "Why")

Multiplication is about Direction.

  • Positive: "Keep going the same way."
  • Negative: "Flip the direction."

Example: \(- \times -\) means "Flip the direction, then flip it again." You end up back where you started: Positive.

2. Addition & Subtraction (The "Why")

Addition is about Position on a number line.

  • Same Signs: You are moving further in the same direction. (Add the values).
  • Different Signs: You are moving in two opposite directions. The "stronger" number wins the sign.

III. Terms vs. Factors (The "Glue" Test)

This determines whether you can "collapse" a fraction or not.

The Factor (Loose)

Linked by Multiplication. These are "unhooked" pieces. You can cancel a factor if it multiplies the entire numerator/denominator.

The Term (Glued)

Linked by Addition or Subtraction. These are "glued" units. You cannot pull a single number out of a sum to cancel it.

IV. Criteria for Cancellation

Cancellation Requirements:
  1. The component must be a Factor (Multiplied by the whole top/bottom).
  2. There must be an Exact Match (Value AND Sign).
  3. Polynomial Units: Any part with a \(+\) or \(-\) must be cancelled as a whole bracket.
Positive Example (Valid): \[\frac{5(x+1)}{5} = x+1\] Why: 5 is a factor multiplying the whole top.
Negative Example (Invalid): \[\frac{5+x+1}{5}\] Why Not: 5 is a term (glued by +). You cannot pull it out.

V. Criteria for using LCM

The LCM Trigger:
  1. The operation is Addition (+) or Subtraction (-).
  2. There are separate fractions.
  3. Denominators are different.
Positive (Need LCM): \[\frac{a}{2} + \frac{b}{3} = \frac{3a+2b}{6}\]
Negative (No LCM): \[\frac{a}{2} \cdot \frac{b}{3} = \frac{ab}{6}\]

(Multiplication just smashes across!)

VI. The Property Cheat Sheet

Commutation: \(a+b = b+a\). Order doesn't change sums/products.
Association: \((a+b)+c = a+(b+c)\). Grouping doesn't change sums.
Distribution: \(a(b+c) = ab + ac\). Factor visits all terms inside.
The Unit Rule: Treat \(x+y\) as a locked box \((x+y)\).
