The Foundation of Algebraic Logic

I. Sign Ownership: Who "Owns" the Sign?

In algebra, signs are not just operations (plus or minus); they are identities that stay with the number.

The Criteria for Sign Belonging:
  1. The "To the Right" Rule: A sign always belongs to the number or variable immediately to its right.
  2. Invisible Positive: If a number is at the very start of a line and has no sign, it is always a Positive (+).
  3. The Bracket Shield: A sign outside a bracket belongs to the entire unit inside.
Correct Identification:

\[a - b + c\]

  • The \(a\) is Positive
  • The \(b\) is Negative
  • The \(c\) is Positive
Negative Example (Common Mistake):

\[10 - 2 + 5\]

Wrong Thinking: "I see \(2+5\), so that's \(7\). Then \(10-7=3\)."

Correct Logic: The sign belongs to the \(2\). It is a Negative 2. The sum is \(10 - 2\) which is \(8\), then \(8 + 5 = 13\).

II. Operations with Signs: The "Why"

1. Multiplication & Division

The Rule: Same signs = Positive (+). Different signs = Negative (-).
The "Why": Multiplication is about Direction.
  • A Positive sign means "Keep the same direction."
  • A Negative sign means "Flip the direction."
  • Flipping twice (\(- \times -\)) brings you back to the start (Positive).

2. Addition & Subtraction

The Rule:
  • Same signs: Add the values, keep the sign.
  • Different signs: Find the difference, keep the sign of the larger absolute value.
The "Why": Addition is about Position on a number line.
  • \(+10 - 12\) means "Go 10 steps Right, then 12 steps Left." You end at \(-2\).

III. Identifying the Structure: Terms vs. Factors

The Factor (Loose)

A component linked by multiplication.

Criteria:
  • Connected by touching or brackets.
  • Must multiply the entire group to be cancelled.

The Term (Glued)

A component linked by addition or subtraction.

Criteria:
  • Separated by \(+\) or \(-\) signs.
  • Cannot be cancelled individually from a unit.

IV. Criteria for Cancellation

Requirements for Cancellation:
  1. The number must be a Factor (multiplying the whole numerator/denominator).
  2. There must be an Exact Match (Sign and Value).
  3. Units (Polynomials) must be cancelled as a whole bracket.
Positive Example (Factor): \[\frac{5(x+1)}{5} = x+1\] Why: 5 is a factor multiplying the whole top.
Negative Example (Term): \[\frac{5+x+1}{5}\] Why Not: 5 is a term. It is "glued" to the \(x\) by a plus sign.

V. Criteria for using LCM

When to use LCM:
  1. Operation is Addition (+) or Subtraction (-).
  2. There are separate fraction bars.
  3. Denominators are different.
Positive Example (LCM): \[\frac{a}{2} + \frac{b}{3} = \frac{3a + 2b}{6}\] Why: Addition between two fractions.
Negative Example (No LCM): \[\frac{a}{2} \cdot \frac{b}{3} = \frac{ab}{6}\] Why Not: Multiplication does not use LCM. Just multiply across.

VI. The Cheat Sheet

Commutation: \(a+b = b+a\). Order doesn't change a sum.
Association: \((a+b)+c = a+(b+c)\). Grouping doesn't change a sum.
Distribution: \(a(b+c) = ab + ac\). Outside factor visits all inside.
The Unit Rule: Treat \(x+y\) as a locked box \((x+y)\).
